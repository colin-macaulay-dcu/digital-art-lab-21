1st – 2nd Class

Create a Character Profile: Roald Dahl's Willy Wonka

Bring a character to life by creating a digital talking portrait and recording their voice.

Students explore the character of Willy Wonka by creating a visual and narrative character profile. They design a portrait incorporating his unique traits and whimsical personality, then bring it to life using the Talkr app to animate and record a voiceover.

Printable version:↓ Download PDF

Step-by-Step Walkthrough

1
Explore the Character

Read or watch a clip from Charlie and the Chocolate Factory. Discuss Wonka's personality — eccentric, inventive, mysterious. What makes him unique? What does he look like? How does he speak?

2
Design the Portrait

Using drawing paper and art materials (or a digital drawing app), create a portrait of Willy Wonka. Think about his top hat, coat, expression, and the colours that represent his character. Add details — chocolate factories, sweets, golden tickets.

3
Set Up in the Talkr App

Take a photo of the finished portrait (or use the digital file). Import it into the Talkr app. Position the mouth and eye markers so the face can be animated.

4
Write & Record the Voiceover

Write a short script in Wonka's voice. What would he say about himself? About his factory? About his favourite invention? Record the voiceover in character — use expression, pacing, and personality.

5
Share & Discuss

Play the talking avatar for the class. Gallery walk or spotlight presentations. Discuss: What did you learn about the character? What artistic choices did you make and why?

Learning Outcomes

1

Creative Expression: Students produce a personalised portrait showcasing Wonka's eccentric traits.

2

Literary Understanding: Students demonstrate understanding of the character's role in the story.

3

Digital Literacy: Students use animation and voice recording tools to bring their portrayal to life.

4

Communication: Students craft and share a narrative voiceover reflecting Wonka's personality.

5

Reflection: Students reflect on how their artistic and narrative choices capture the character's essence.

3rd – 4th Class

Create an Artist Profile: The Movement of Fauvism

Create a digital character profile of a Fauvist artist, combining their artistic style and personal story.

Students explore the bold art movement of Fauvism by creating a fictional artist inspired by its principles. They design a portrait using exaggerated colours and simplified forms, then animate their artist and record a voiceover expressing their character's artistic philosophy.

Printable version:↓ Download PDF

Step-by-Step Walkthrough

1
Research Fauvism

Explore the Fauvist movement — Matisse, Derain, Vlaminck. Look at examples of bold colour, simplified forms, and emotional expression. Discuss: What makes Fauvism different? Why were they called "wild beasts"?

2
Create Your Fauvist Artist

Invent a fictional Fauvist artist. Give them a name, backstory, and artistic philosophy. Design their portrait using chalk pastels, bright markers, or digital tools — bold colours, exaggerated features, simplified forms.

3
Set Up in the Talkr App

Photo or import the portrait into Talkr. Set up the animation markers. Test the mouth and eye movement.

4
Write & Record the Voiceover

Write a script as the artist. What is their philosophy? What inspires them? Why do they paint the way they do? Record in character.

5
Share & Discuss

Present the talking avatar. Peer feedback — what Fauvist elements are visible? What did you learn about the movement?

Learning Outcomes

1

Creative Expression: A vivid Fauvist-inspired portrait capturing the essence of the movement.

2

Art Historical Understanding: Knowledge of Fauvism — bold colours, simplified forms, emotional expression.

3

Digital Literacy: Using animation and voice recording to bring a Fauvist artist to life.

4

Communication: Articulating the artist's backstory and philosophy through voiceover.

5

Reflection: Reflecting on artistic choices and the emotional impact of Fauvist techniques.

3rd – 4th Class

Create a Titanic Passenger Profile

Create a digital talking portrait of an imaginary Titanic passenger, bringing their story to life.

Students create a fictional Titanic passenger — combining historical context with creative storytelling. They design a digital portrait considering class, background, and motivations, then animate their character using the Talkr app and record a voiceover backstory.

Printable version:↓ Download PDF

Step-by-Step Walkthrough

1
Research the Titanic

Explore the history of the Titanic — who was on board? First class, second class, third class. What were their reasons for travelling? Look at historical images and discuss the social structure of the time.

2
Create Your Passenger

Invent a fictional passenger. Decide their name, age, class, background, and reason for travelling. Design their portrait — considering clothing, expression, and era-appropriate details.

3
Set Up in the Talkr App

Import the portrait into Talkr and set up the animation markers.

4
Write & Record the Voiceover

Write a first-person script as the passenger. Who are they? Where are they going? What are their hopes and fears? Record in character.

5
Share & Discuss

Present to the class. Discuss historical accuracy, creative choices, and what the characters reveal about life in 1912.

Learning Outcomes

1

Creative Expression: A personalised Titanic character portrait reflecting understanding of the era.

2

Historical Understanding: Knowledge of the Titanic's social structure and historical significance.

3

Digital Literacy: Using digital drawing and animation tools to design and animate a character.

4

Communication: Crafting a backstory through a recorded voiceover.

5

Reflection: Connecting creative work to historical themes and passenger experiences.

5th – 6th Class

Create a Viking Warrior Profile

Bring a Viking warrior character to life by creating a digital talking portrait and sharing their story.

Students create a Viking warrior, explorer, or craftsperson — combining historical understanding with artistic expression. They design and paint a portrait using traditional art materials, then bring the character to life with the Talkr app to animate and record a voiceover.

Printable version:↓ Download PDF

Step-by-Step Walkthrough

1
Research the Vikings

Explore Viking culture — warriors, explorers, traders, craftspeople. What did they wear? What were their beliefs? Look at Viking art patterns, runes, and ship designs. Discuss their role in Irish history.

2
Create Your Viking Character

Choose a role — warrior, explorer, craftsperson, chieftain. Design their portrait using traditional art materials — paint, pastels, ink. Include Viking-era details: armour, jewellery, symbols, patterns.

3
Set Up in the Talkr App

Photograph the traditional artwork and import into Talkr. Set up animation markers.

4
Write & Record the Voiceover

Write a script as the Viking character. What is their name? Where have they travelled? What battles have they fought? What do they believe in? Record in character.

5
Share & Discuss

Present to the class. Gallery walk. Discuss historical accuracy, how traditional and digital art worked together, and what they learned about Viking culture.

Learning Outcomes

1

Creative Expression: A unique Viking character portrait showcasing bold artistic choices.

2

Historical Understanding: Knowledge of Viking roles, culture, and motivations through character design.

3

Digital Literacy: Animating and voicing a Viking character, combining traditional art with technology.

4

Communication: Presenting the character to peers, articulating their story and cultural significance.

5

Reflection: Connecting art and history meaningfully through creative choices.

Teaching Toolkit

Supporting information for all four lessons — tap a tab to explore.

Key Competencies — Irish Primary Curriculum

💡 Being Creative

Encourages imaginative thinking, innovative use of digital tools, and creative problem-solving through character design and storytelling.

🗣️ Being a Communicator

The voiceover activity develops oral communication skills — clarity, expressive language, and structured presentation.

📱 Being a Digital Learner

Students gain hands-on experience with animation and voice recording tools, building confidence in using digital technology creatively.

🌍 Cultural & Cross-Curricular

By exploring artists, historical figures, and literary characters, students engage with history, literacy, SPHE, and cultural awareness.

HIGHER-ORDER THINKING

  • Why do you think this character acts the way they do? What shaped them?
  • If your character could give advice to someone today, what would they say?
  • How might your character's experiences have changed them over time?
  • What emotions did you try to show through your artistic choices?
  • How could art and technology help us understand historical figures or fictional characters?
  • If you could meet your character, what would you ask them?
  • How might your character's story be different if they lived in today's world?

LOWER-ORDER THINKING

  • Who is your character? What do they look like?
  • What is their name and where do they come from?
  • What do they do? What is their job or role?
  • What colours did you use and why?
  • What does your character say in their voiceover?
  • What materials did you use to create your portrait?
  • What did you enjoy most about this activity?
📁

Digital Portfolios

Students collect and curate their portrait, script, and animated voiceover as a digital portfolio entry — evidence of process and outcome.

💬

Peer Feedback

Gallery walks, group rotations, or spotlight presentations allow students to give and receive constructive feedback.

🔍

Reflection & Critique

Students reflect on their artistic and narrative choices — what worked, what they'd do differently, and how their work connects to the theme.

👁️

Teacher Observation

Observations focus on creativity, technique, and presentation — assessed on individual effort and progress rather than uniform standards.

CONTENT

Vary the Resources

Offer simplified summaries or video clips for students needing support. Provide in-depth texts or primary sources for advanced learners.

PROCESS

Scaffold the Steps

Use character profile templates with sentence starters for less confident students. Offer open-ended prompts for advanced learners. Pair students for peer support.

PRODUCT

Flexible Output

Less confident: a simple portrait and short structured script. Advanced: a detailed portrait with elaborate backstory and longer voiceover. All outputs are valued equally.

ENVIRONMENT

Station-Based Support

Set up a guided station with teacher support, an independent station for confident learners, and a tech help station for the app.

ASSESSMENT

Individual Progress

Assess based on effort, creativity, and engagement — not uniform standards. Simple reflection questions for less confident learners; deeper critical reflection for advanced.

EXTENSION

Going Further

Challenge advanced students to design a new invention (Wonka), create a philosophical motto (Fauvism), add dramatic Titanic storytelling, or include Viking symbolic patterns.

ART MATERIALS

  • Paper: A3 or A4 drawing paper, sketchbooks, or cardstock
  • Sketching: Pencils, erasers, and sharpeners
  • Colour: Coloured pencils, markers, watercolours, acrylic paints
  • Chalk Pastels: Bright, bold colours — ideal for Fauvism activity
  • Collage (optional): Coloured paper, scissors, glue sticks
  • Markers: Fine-tip black markers for outlines and details
  • Reference images: Visual examples relevant to each activity's theme

DIGITAL TOOLS & HARDWARE

  • iPads or Tablets: One per student or per pair — fully charged
  • Talkr App: Pre-installed and updated on all devices
  • Headphones with microphones: For recording voiceovers
  • Styluses (optional): For more precise digital drawing
  • Classroom Wi-Fi: Stable connection for app use
  • Drawing apps (optional): Procreate, Tayasui Sketches, or Canva
  • Projector / Whiteboard: For teacher demonstrations
